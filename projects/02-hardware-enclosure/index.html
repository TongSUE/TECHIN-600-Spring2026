01 Overview

The main project

The main project for this quarter is to take a target hardware enclosure and redesign it from scratch with full DFM principles — meaning the design must be able to be actually manufactured via casting and injection molding, not just 3D printed.

This involves CAD work (defining parting lines, adding draft angles, designing internal mounting bosses and snap-fits), creating a master pattern via SLA printing, making a professional two-part silicone mold, casting the enclosure in resin, and finally assembling and post-processing the result.

02 Theory

DFM principles & references

Design for Manufacturing means designing parts so they can be efficiently and reliably made using a specific process. For casting and injection molding, several rules govern what geometry is possible and what isn't.

"A part that can't be demolded isn't a part — it's a permanent installation." The parting line and draft angles are the first things to design, not the last.

Draft angles — vertical walls perpendicular to the parting direction will lock into the mold. A minimum of 1°–3° draft on all surfaces allows clean demolding without tearing the silicone or stressing the part.

Parting line — the seam where the two mold halves meet. Its placement determines what's visible on the final part and what geometry can be captured in each half. Good parting line design is as much an aesthetic decision as a functional one.

Wall thickness — for resin casting, 2–4 mm is typical. Too thin and the part is fragile; too thick and you risk exothermic heat buildup during cure (polyurethane resins generate heat as they cure — a large mass can crack).

Mounting bosses & snap-fits — instead of screws or glue, well-designed snap-fits allow assembly and disassembly without tools. Boss diameter is typically 2× the screw diameter; boss height should not exceed 3× its diameter.

04 Knowledge Points

Things to remember

Draft angle rule of thumb: 1° minimum, 3° preferred

More draft = easier demolding. Less draft = better appearance. The balance depends on texture: textured surfaces need more draft than polished ones.

Parting line placement affects part aesthetics

Mold seam lines are always visible to some degree. Place the parting line at a natural edge or shadow line on the design so it's least noticeable.

Boss design ratio: height ≤ 3× diameter

Tall, thin bosses are fragile and hard to demold. Keep the height-to-diameter ratio under 3:1. Add gussets to bosses that experience lateral loads.

Vent holes prevent air traps in two-part molds

Any cavity in the mold that air can't escape from will create a void in the cast part. Small vent channels at the highest points of each mold half solve this.
