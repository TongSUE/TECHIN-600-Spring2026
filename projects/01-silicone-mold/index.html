01 Overview

The starting point

Before working on the main custom enclosure, Kevin had me start with a simple experiment: pick an existing physical object, make a silicone mold of it, and cast a copy using two-part resin. The goal wasn't a perfect copy — it was to get comfortable with the material behavior and workflow before anything was at stake.

I chose a phone case (not designed by me) because it has a mix of curved surfaces, thin walls, and some surface texture — interesting enough to learn from, simple enough to not overthink.

Materials Used

Platinum-cure silicone (Part A + B) Two-part polyurethane resin Mold release spray Mixing cups & stir sticks Phone case (existing object) Cardboard mold box Hot glue

02 Theory

What I read & learned

Kevin shared some foundational reading on silicone mold-making before we started. The key ideas I took away:

"Platinum-cure (addition-cure) silicones are preferred for product work because they don't shrink as much, have better detail capture, and are compatible with most resins. Tin-cure silicones are cheaper but shrink over time."

Why silicone? Silicone is flexible, self-releasing for most resins, and captures fine surface detail accurately. It can be poured around an object and demolded without damaging the original.

Open-face vs two-part molds. An open-face mold is the simplest: you build a box around the object, pour silicone over the top, and let it cure. You can only cast objects with one flat side. A two-part mold allows both sides to be captured, but requires designing a parting line and registration marks so the two halves align. For this experiment, open-face was the right choice.

Resin basics. Two-part polyurethane resins cure by chemical reaction (not evaporation). Mix ratio must be exact — usually 1:1 or 1:2 by volume. Pot life (working time) is typically 3–5 minutes for fast-cure resins; full cure is 30 min–24 hours. Air bubbles are the main enemy — they get trapped during mixing and pouring.

03 Process

Step by step

1

Build the mold box

I built a simple box out of cardboard, sized about 1 cm larger than the phone case on each side to give the silicone enough wall thickness. Hot-glued all seams to prevent leaks. Placed the phone case face-up and secured it to the base so it wouldn't float when the silicone was poured.

📷 Mold box setup
📷 Phone case placed inside
2

Mix & pour silicone

Mixed Part A and Part B at a 1:1 ratio by weight. Stirred slowly for 3 minutes, scraping the sides of the cup. Poured in a thin stream from a corner of the box to reduce air bubbles — the silicone flows around the object and any trapped air rises to the surface.

📷 Mixing silicone
📷 Pouring into mold box
3

Cure & demold

Let cure at room temperature for 16 hours (overnight). Tore apart the cardboard box, then slowly peeled the silicone mold away from the phone case. The silicone stretched around the undercuts without tearing — the flexibility is key here.

📷 Finished silicone mold after demolding
4

Cast resin

Sprayed a light coat of mold release into the silicone mold. Mixed two-part resin at 1:1 by volume. Poured slowly into the mold. Pot life was about 3 minutes so speed mattered. Left to cure for 1 hour before demolding.

📷 Cast result after demolding

04 Knowledge Points

Things to remember

Mix by weight, not just volume

For platinum silicones, weight is more accurate than eyeballing volume — the two parts often have different densities. A kitchen scale is worth it.

Pour thin and slow to avoid bubbles

A thin, high stream of silicone breaks up any bubbles forming in the cup. Pour from a corner, not directly onto the object. Tilting the mold box while pouring helps it flow under crevices.

Silicone wall thickness ≥ 10 mm at the thinnest point

Thinner than 10 mm and the mold tears easily, especially around sharp detail. Over-sizing the mold box is safer than under-sizing.

Inhibition: platinum silicones are sensitive to contamination

Sulfur-containing materials (some clays, latex gloves, certain foams) can inhibit platinum-cure silicone — it stays sticky and never fully cures. Use nitrile gloves. Test unfamiliar materials with a small batch first.

Mold release is essential for resin casting

Without mold release, polyurethane resin can bond to the silicone permanently. A thin, even coat of spray release is enough. Let it flash off (30 seconds) before pouring.
