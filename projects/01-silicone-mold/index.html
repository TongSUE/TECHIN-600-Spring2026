01 Overview

The starting point

Before jumping into the main enclosure project, Kevin had me start with a practice run: pick an existing physical object, make a silicone mold of it, and cast a copy. The goal wasn't a perfect duplicate — it was to get hands-on experience with material behavior and workflow before anything critical was on the line.

I chose a phone case. Kevin's honest assessment: it's a tricky first object — thin walls, hollow cutouts for buttons and a large camera opening. But that's exactly what made it interesting. Challenge accepted.

Materials Used

Tin-cure silicone (A:B = 1:10 by weight) Two-part polyurethane resin (A:B = 1:1 by volume) Cherry plywood (laser-cut, 2 layers per wall) Cardboard & hot glue (mold box rebuild) Tape + acrylic sheet (hole blocking) Air-dry clay (prototype ballast) Vaseline (parting agent) Glitter pigments Vacuum degassing chamber Digital scale, beakers, stir sticks Craft knife + sandpaper (post-processing)

Key things I took away from the reading:

Tin-cure vs. platinum-cure silicone. We used tin-cure here — it's cheaper and more accessible, though it shrinks slightly over time. Platinum-cure is preferred for high-precision production work (better dimensional stability, no shrinkage). For a learning exercise, tin-cure is perfectly fine.

The mixing ratio is everything. Tin-cure silicone is A:B = 1:10 by weight. Part A is the catalyst — the manufacturer dyes it pink so you can visually confirm uniform mixing. Too much catalyst → pot life drops sharply and the silicone sets before you finish pouring. Too little → it may not fully cure for days. With the correct ratio, you have about 40 minutes of working time.

Degassing removes trapped air. Before pouring, the mixed silicone goes into a vacuum chamber. As pressure drops, trapped bubbles expand and rise to the surface — the silicone visibly expands to several times its original volume. You release pressure intermittently to prevent it from overflowing the container. When it starts collapsing on its own without venting, the air is out.

Resin basics. Two-part polyurethane resin cures by chemical reaction, not evaporation. Mix ratio must be exact — 1:1 by volume for this resin. Pot life is short, so once you start mixing, move with intention. Incomplete mixing and trapped air bubbles are the main failure modes.

03 Process

Step by step

1

Build the mold box

I laser-cut the mold box sides from cherry plywood. Because the individual sheets were a bit thin, I stacked two layers per wall — the added thickness keeps the walls vertical and gives the box enough rigidity to hold its shape when heavy silicone is poured in. Sealed all seams and joints with hot glue.

2

Pre-process the prototype

The phone case has several openings — button cutouts, charging port, and a large camera hole. Left unsealed, silicone would flow into these gaps and lock the case inside the mold permanently, making it impossible to demold cleanly.

I taped over the smaller holes. For the camera opening, I cut a thin acrylic sheet to cover it — tape alone wasn't rigid enough for such a wide span.

3

Fix the prototype in the box

Kevin suggested filling the inside of the phone case with air-dry clay before placing it in the mold box.

Two reasons this helps:

  1. The clay adds weight and surface contact — combined with its natural stickiness, it anchors the case to the base board so it won't float or shift when the silicone is poured in.
  2. It seals the case edges more thoroughly. Tape is thin; the clay fills any gaps that could let air escape and create bubbles in the mold.

With the clay in place, I inverted the phone case and pressed it face-down onto the base board. Kevin also recommended adding a few wood strip registration bosses around the perimeter — ideally you'd use tapered alignment pins for better guidance, but the laser cutter can't produce undercuts, so simple strips work as a basic guide for aligning the two mold halves later.

4

Mix & degas the silicone

Messy, careful work — lay down waste paper, gloves on, cleanup tools ready. We used tin-cure silicone at a 1:10 A:B ratio by weight.

A tip Kevin shared: write Part B's weight directly on the waste paper while you're measuring it, so you have a quick reference when you measure Part A. (You'll thank yourself when you forget the number mid-pour — I definitely would have.)

Stir continuously until the mixture is a completely uniform color — no streaks, no color bands, especially at the cup edges and walls. Any visible inconsistency means unmixed catalyst, which creates soft or uncured spots in the finished mold.

Then into the vacuum degassing chamber. The green valve (left) connects to the pump; the red valve (right) vents to the atmosphere. Close red, open green, start the pump — watch the pressure gauge drop and the silicone begin to expand dramatically. When it approaches the rim, crack the red valve slightly to let it collapse, then close and continue pumping. When the silicone starts collapsing on its own without venting — the bubbles are gone. Close the green valve, wait a couple of minutes, then slowly open the red to re-equalize before opening the chamber.

5

Pour the bottom half — cure overnight

Pour from one corner of the mold box. Raise the container as high as comfortably possible and let the stream run thin — the narrow stream naturally breaks up any remaining bubbles, and the silicone flows around the object on its own, filling every corner.

Then: wait. Tin-cure silicone takes about 8 hours at room temperature.

6

Demold the bottom half & prep for the top

Once cured, tear apart the mold box walls and carefully lift out the bottom half with the phone case still embedded in it.

Remove the clay and tape. Then the critical step: coat every exposed silicone surface with vaseline — the flat parting face, inside every hole recess, and around every registration boss impression. Don't miss a single spot. Any uncovered silicone-to-silicone contact during the second pour will fuse the two halves together permanently.

Trim any rough edges with scissors or a craft knife. Set the phone case face-up, rebuild the mold box walls around the bottom half, and seal with hot glue.

7

Pour the top half & split the mold

Second silicone pour, same technique — corner pour from height. Another ~8 hours of waiting.

Tear down the box again, then separate the two halves. The vaseline does its job — they peel apart cleanly without sticking.

8

Mix resin & cast

AB resin, 1:1 by volume. For this mold, about 2–3 oz total.

The most fun part: adding glitter. I mixed white, light blue, and deep blue glitter with a small touch of gold for depth. It felt a bit like mixing nail polish colors — unexpectedly satisfying.

Mix vigorously for about 5 minutes. Same discipline as the silicone — pay extra attention to cup edges and bottom.

Pour resin into the bottom mold half — deliberately overfill it. Press the top half down steadily so the excess resin squeezes out from the edges naturally. Transfer the closed mold to a fresh sheet of waste paper (to prevent the overflow resin from gluing to the table while still wet). Wait ~24 hours.

04 Knowledge Points

Things to remember

Weigh the silicone — every gram matters

For tin-cure silicone, the A:B ratio is 1:10 by weight. Part A is the catalyst. Too much → pot life drops sharply, the silicone sets before you finish pouring. Too little → may not fully cure for days. A kitchen scale, and writing the target weight on your waste paper before you start, both help.

Pour from a corner, raise the container high

A thin silicone stream from height naturally pops bubbles as it falls. Pouring directly onto the object traps air underneath. Flow from a corner and let the silicone find its own way around. Tilting the mold box slightly while pouring helps it reach tight spots.

Silicone wall thickness ≥ 10 mm at the thinnest point

Thinner than 10 mm and the mold tears easily, especially around tight detail. Oversize your mold box — silicone is the cheap part compared to a ruined mold.

Degas before every pour

The vacuum chamber is not optional. Even a well-stirred mix has microscopic bubbles. Degassing visibly expands and bursts them. Release pressure intermittently to prevent the silicone from overflowing; when it collapses on its own without prompting, the air is out.

Vaseline keeps the two mold halves from bonding — don't miss a spot

Any exposed silicone-to-silicone contact during the second pour will fuse permanently. Coat the entire parting face, every hole recess, every registration impression. A missed patch means a mold you can't open.

05 Notes & Reflection

What I took away

Week 1 – 2 · Spring 2026

Kevin was right that a phone case would be a tricky first object. The thin walls and the multiple cutouts made it genuinely difficult. But those difficulties turned out to be the most useful part — they forced me to think about every step rather than just following a recipe.

The overall pace was slower and more methodical than I expected. You commit to a step and then wait overnight, twice. Mistakes only reveal themselves after the silicone has already cured. The vacuum degassing was the most satisfying moment — watching the silicone visibly expand and then slowly collapse as the air left was surprisingly visual and almost meditative.

Adding the glitter to the resin was genuinely fun. The white + light blue + deep blue + touch of gold combination came together with a depth I didn't predict — somewhere between frosted glass and sandy mahjong tiles. I didn't expect to enjoy that step as much as I did.

The final result surprised me. It actually works as a phone case. It fits, it's usable, it's mine. Way beyond what I expected for a first attempt.

One result that genuinely impressed me: silicone captures fine surface detail remarkably well. The tiny rabbit emboss on the original case was replicated in full.

  • ✓ What worked: The two mold halves separated cleanly. Glitter distribution is even and looks intentional. Most surprisingly — the tiny rabbit emboss was fully replicated in the cast.
  • △ What I'd do differently: Apply more consistent, firm pressure when closing the mold halves — the slightly-thick bottom and the air pocket likely came from uneven force. Also degas the resin before pouring, not just the silicone.
  • → What this taught me for the main project: Surface detail transfers remarkably well through silicone. Registration features need to be more precisely designed (tapered is better than flat strips). Always budget time for post-processing — knife, sandpaper, and patience are part of the process.
